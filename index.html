April 11, 1945, in Wolfenbüttel Defeat, Liberation, and New Beginnings in the Municipality

With the surrender of the Wehrmacht, World War II in Europe came to an end on May 8, 1945. As a consequence of Germany’s war of annihilation, millions of lives were lost. Never before had a war caused such an immense scale of destruction. Military operations, occupation, mass crimes, forced displacement, expulsion, and resettlement inflicted profound and lasting suffering on people across the continent. For Wolfenbüttel, the war ended earlier, on April 11, 1945, with the arrival of the U.S. 9th Army—almost four weeks before Germany’s final surrender. As in other German cities, the local population had not liberated itself from National Socialism. Nevertheless, the Allied forces were able to take control of the former ducal residence without any combat. The American forces, and later the British from the summer of 1945 onward, ensured that Wolfenbüttel successfully transitioned from dictatorship to a democracy modeled after the Western system. Marking the 80th anniversary of the war’s end and the liberation of Wolfenbüttel Prison, this exhibition explores the final days of National Socialist tyranny, the hardships and shortages endured by the civilian population in the postwar period, the challenges posed by the arrival of approximately 11,000 refugees and displaced persons, and the democratic rebirth that signaled the beginning of a new era.
